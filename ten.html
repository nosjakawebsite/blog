The Luzhin Defense by Vladimir Nabokov

★★★★☆

The Luzhin Defense by Vladimir Nabokov tells a crazy story about obsession and the delicacy of a life built around one single passion. Luzhin, who in my opinion is a near genius in the world of chess and the only thing he knows in life, becomes so ultimately obsessed and immersed in the game he starts seeing life entirely through its patterns and strategies. For example, imagine using the Nimzowitsch-Larsen Attack or the Scandinavian Defense (chess openings) as a way of approaching life and its random interactions. Ultimately, when you're forced to step away from your obsession for too long, it can lead to your downfall, which is exactly what happened to Luzhin in the story.

Nabokov uses chess in such a brilliant metaphor for art and how it connects to life. He is able to illustrate how both require immense strength, creativity, and focus. He explores how art, like chess, can offer structure and meaning in your life but also demand complete affection. The metaphor shows the beauty and danger of losing yourself in the pursuit of perfection.

During the book, many political themes appeared within the background unexpectedly. In my opinion, this adds a lot to the story, showing the societal changes during Soviet-era Russia and Berlin. A good example of this is Luzhin’s mother-in-law at the time calling chess players mad and worthless compared to others with real jobs.

Overall, I think The Luzhin Defense by Vladimir Nabokov is an amazing piece of art that I would recommend anyone to read. Would read again!
